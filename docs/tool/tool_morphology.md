# **Word Morphology Guide**

This tool provides detailed grammatical analysis of Hebrew and Greek words in the original languages, allowing you to study the linguistic structure of Scripture at the word level.

## **What is Word Morphology?**

Word Morphology displays the grammatical form and structure of each Hebrew (Old Testament) or Greek (New Testament) word in a Bible verse. This includes information about parts of speech, verb tenses, noun cases, and other grammatical features that affect meaning.

## **How to Use the Morphology Tool**

1. **Select a Verse**: Use the Bible selector to choose a specific verse.
2. **Click "Go"**: Load the morphological analysis for that verse.
3. **View Word Data**: Each word is displayed with its form, lexical entry, and morphological tags.
4. **Select Morphology**: Click morphology chips to select/deselect grammatical features for searching.
5. **Search**: Click a search button to find verses with similar morphological patterns.

## **Word Display**

Each word in the verse is shown in a card with four main elements:

### **1. Original Text**

- **Hebrew Words**: Displayed right-to-left with proper formatting
- **Greek Words**: Displayed in Unicode Greek characters
- **Hover Tooltips**: Hover over words to see lexical information

### **2. Lexeme (Dictionary Form)**

- The base form of the word as it appears in lexicons
- Clickable with tooltip for additional information
- Links to the word's lexical entry

### **3. Interlinear Translation**

- Word-for-word English translation
- Helps understand the basic meaning
- Shown between the original text and morphology

### **4. Morphological Tags**

Selectable chips showing grammatical features such as:
- **Part of Speech**: Noun, Verb, Adjective, etc.
- **Person**: 1st, 2nd, 3rd person
- **Gender**: Masculine, Feminine, Neuter
- **Number**: Singular, Plural, Dual
- **Case**: Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative (Greek)
- **State**: Absolute, Construct (Hebrew)
- **Tense**: Present, Aorist, Perfect, Imperfect, etc.
- **Voice**: Active, Middle, Passive
- **Mood**: Indicative, Subjunctive, Imperative, etc.

## **Search Features**

### **Morphological Pattern Search**

After viewing a word's morphology, you can search for similar patterns:

1. **Select Morphology Tags**: Click orange chips to select/deselect grammatical features
2. **Choose a Bible Version**: Click one of the search buttons:
   - **OHGB**: Original Hebrew and Greek Bible
   - **OHGBi**: Interlinear version
   - **Current Bible**: The Bible version currently open in your Bible area
   - **Primary Bible**: Your preferred primary translation
   - **Secondary Bible**: Your preferred secondary translation

3. **Run Search**: The search finds all verses where the word appears with your selected morphological features

**Example**: Search for all occurrences of a specific Greek verb in the aorist tense, passive voice.

### **Advanced Search Options**

- **Lexical Entry Search**: Search for all forms of a specific word (any morphology)
- **Combined Search**: Select multiple morphological features to narrow results
- **Version-Specific**: Search in original languages or translations

## **Understanding Morphological Tags**

### **Common Hebrew Tags**

- **verb**: Verb
- **noun**: Noun
- **qal**: Simple verb stem
- **perfect**: Completed action
- **construct**: Construct state (relation to following word)
- **masculine/feminine**: Gender
- **singular/plural**: Number

### **Common Greek Tags**

- **verb**: Verb
- **noun**: Noun
- **aorist**: Simple past action
- **present**: Ongoing action
- **indicative**: Statement of fact
- **imperative**: Command
- **nominative**: Subject case
- **accusative**: Direct object case
- **active/passive**: Voice

## **Example Workflows**

### **Greek Verb Study**

1. Load John 3:16
2. Find the verb "loved" (ἠγάπησεν)
3. Note it's aorist tense, active voice, indicative mood
4. Select "aorist" and "active" chips
5. Search OHGB to find other aorist active uses of ἀγαπάω
6. Study how this form emphasizes completed action

### **Hebrew Noun Analysis**

1. Load Genesis 1:1
2. Examine אֱלֹהִים (Elohim - God)
3. Note it's masculine plural but grammatically singular
4. Click "Lexicon" to read about this unique form
5. Use concordance to see where else this form appears

### **Comparative Form Study**

1. Load a verse with a key verb
2. Note the morphological tags
3. Search in OHGB for that exact form
4. Switch to primary Bible and search again
5. Compare how different translations handle the same grammatical form
